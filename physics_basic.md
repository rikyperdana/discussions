I want to learn physics from basic to intermediate level
Okay, that's a great goal! Learning physics is incredibly rewarding. Here's a structured plan to take you from basic to intermediate levels, including resources, topics, and tips.  I'll break it down into phases and suggest resources for each.

**Phase 1: Foundational Physics (High School Level - Introductory)**

*   **Goal:** Build a solid understanding of the core concepts.  This is the bedrock upon which everything else is built.
*   **Topics:**
    *   **Mechanics:** Kinematics (motion), Dynamics (forces, Newton's Laws), Work, Energy, Power, Momentum, Collisions, Gravitation.
    *   **Heat and Thermodynamics:** Temperature, Heat Transfer (conduction, convection, radiation), Laws of Thermodynamics, Heat Engines.
    *   **Waves:**  Wave properties (wavelength, frequency, amplitude), Types of waves (transverse, longitudinal), Superposition, Interference, Diffraction, Doppler Effect.
    *   **Electricity and Magnetism:** Electric Charge, Electric Fields, Electric Potential, Capacitance, Current, Resistance, Ohm's Law, Circuits (series, parallel), Magnetism, Magnetic Fields, Electromagnetic Induction.
    *   **Optics:** Reflection, Refraction, Lenses, Mirrors, Interference, Diffraction.
*   **Resources:**
    *   **Khan Academy Physics:** (https://www.khanacademy.org/science/physics)  Excellent, free, and comprehensive.  Covers all the topics above with videos, practice problems, and articles.  A fantastic starting point.
    *   **Physics for Scientists and Engineers by Serway and Jewett:** A very popular and well-regarded textbook.  It's a bit more detailed than Khan Academy, but a good reference. (Often available used).
    *   **HyperPhysics:** (http://hyperphysics.phy-astr.gsu.edu/hbase/hframe.html) A great online resource with a lot of concise explanations and diagrams.
    *   **Walter Lewin's MIT Physics Lectures (YouTube):** (https://www.youtube.com/@walterlewin)  Highly engaging and insightful lectures.  A bit fast-paced, but very rewarding.  Focus on the introductory lectures.
    *   **OpenStax College Physics:** (https://openstax.org/details/books/physics) Free, open-source textbook.

*   **How to Learn:**
    *   **Start with Khan Academy:**  Work through the introductory sections on each topic.  Don't just watch the videos; actively try the practice problems.
    *   **Take Notes:**  Summarize the key concepts in your own words.
    *   **Practice, Practice, Practice:**  Work through as many problems as you can.  The more you practice, the better you'll understand the concepts.
    *   **Don't be afraid to ask for help:**  If you're stuck, ask a friend, a teacher, or post a question on a physics forum (like Physics Forums).



**Phase 2: Intermediate Physics (Introductory College Level)**

*   **Goal:**  Deepen your understanding of the foundational concepts and introduce more advanced topics.  Develop problem-solving skills.
*   **Topics:**
    *   **Mechanics (Advanced):**  Rotational Motion, Rigid Body Dynamics,  Oscillations (Simple Harmonic Motion, Damped Oscillations),  Fluid Mechanics (Pressure, Buoyancy, Fluid Dynamics).
    *   **Thermodynamics (Advanced):** Statistical Mechanics (brief introduction), Entropy,  Heat Engines (efficiency), Refrigeration.
    *   **Electromagnetism:**  Electrostatics (Gauss's Law, Electric Potential), Magnetostatics (Biot-Savart Law, Ampere's Law), Electromagnetic Waves,  Maxwell's Equations (introduction).
    *   **Modern Physics (Introduction):**  Special Relativity (time dilation, length contraction),  Quantum Mechanics (brief introduction to wave-particle duality, Schrödinger equation).
*   **Resources:**
    *   **University Physics with Modern Physics by Young and Freedman:**  A standard college-level physics textbook.  More rigorous than Serway and Jewett.
    *   **MIT OpenCourseWare (8.01 and 8.02):** (https://ocw.mit.edu/courses/physics/)  Free lecture notes, problem sets, and exams from MIT's introductory physics courses.  Excellent if you want a more challenging and in-depth experience.
    *   **Walter Lewin's MIT Physics Lectures (YouTube):** Continue watching his lectures, focusing on the intermediate topics.
    *   **HyperPhysics:**  Still a valuable resource for definitions and concepts.
    *   **Physics Forums:** (https://www.physicsforums.com/)  A great place to ask questions and get help from other physics students.
    *   **MIT Open Learning:** (https://open.mit.edu/courses/physics/) Offers a variety of physics courses, some of which are free.
*   **How to Learn:**
    *   **Work through the textbook systematically:**  Read the chapters carefully and work through the example problems.
    *   **Do all the practice problems:**  Don't just do the ones assigned in the textbook; find additional problems online.
    *   **Attend online lectures or watch video lectures:**  This can help you understand the concepts better.
    *   **Join a study group:**  Working with other students can help you stay motivated and learn from each other.
    *   **Focus on problem-solving:**  Physics is about solving problems.  Practice as many problems as you can.



**Tips for Success:**

*   **Consistency is key:**  Try to study physics regularly, even if it's just for 30 minutes a day.
*   **Don't be afraid to ask for help:**  If you're stuck, ask a friend, a teacher, or post a question on a physics forum.
*   **Visualize the concepts:**  Physics is often about visualizing things.  Try to draw diagrams and pictures to help you understand the concepts.
*   **Relate physics to the real world:**  Look for examples of physics in everyday life.  This can help you make the concepts more meaningful.
*   **Be patient:**  Learning physics takes time and effort.  Don't get discouraged if you don't understand everything right away.
*   **Find a learning style that works for you:** Some people learn best by reading, others by watching videos, and others by doing practice problems. Experiment to find what works best for you.
*   **Don't just memorize formulas:**  Understand *why* the formulas work.  This will help you apply them to new problems.
