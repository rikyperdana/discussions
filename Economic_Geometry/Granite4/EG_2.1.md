### Sub-Chapter: Basic Concepts in Geometry (Points, Lines, Planes, Shapes)

Geometry is the branch of mathematics concerned with the shapes and forms of objects. It provides us with the tools to describe the world around us, from the smallest atom to the vast expanse of galaxies. In economics, these geometric concepts are not just abstract; they are essential for understanding how economies function and evolve over time.

#### Points
A point is an undefined term in geometry but can be thought of as a location with no size or dimension—just a position in space. In economic terms, points often represent specific data points on graphs, such as the price of a commodity at a given time or the employment rate across different regions. These discrete locations are crucial for analyzing trends and making predictions about future movements.

**Example:** Consider the historical stock prices of Apple Inc. Each recorded price point is a point in a two-dimensional space where one axis represents time (e.g., months, years) and the other represents price levels (on a logarithmic scale to accommodate vast differences).

#### Lines
Lines are one-dimensional figures that extend infinitely in both directions. They can be straight or curved and serve as boundaries between different regions of interest. In economics, lines often represent trends over time, such as the trajectory of inflation rates or economic growth.

**Example:** A line graph depicting monthly unemployment rates across a country would show how these rates change year after year. The slope of this line could indicate whether employment conditions are improving (positive slope) or deteriorating (negative slope).

#### Planes
A plane is a flat, two-dimensional surface that extends infinitely in all directions. It can be thought of as the floor of an infinite room with no walls. Economically speaking, planes might represent entire markets or sectors where various products and services interact. For instance, consider the market for smartphones: this plane encompasses not just individual sales but also manufacturing costs, distribution networks, consumer preferences, and technological advancements.

**Example:** A two-dimensional map of global trade routes can illustrate how goods move across different planes (countries) and regions (economies). This visual helps identify key players in international commerce and potential chokepoints for economic activity.

#### Shapes
Shapes are formed by combining lines and curves. They come in various forms, such as triangles, circles, or polygons, each with unique properties that influence their behavior when applied to economic contexts. For instance, the shape of a demand curve (typically downward sloping) reveals how consumers react to price changes—a fundamental concept in market dynamics.

**Example:** A supply and demand graph is essentially two curves (upward for supply, downward for demand) plotted on the same plane. Their intersection determines equilibrium prices and quantities, showcasing how these shapes interact within an economic system.
